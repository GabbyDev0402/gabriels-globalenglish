Mastering English Prepositions: A Comprehensive Guide

Hey everyone! Let's talk prepositions – those little words like "on," "at," "in," "to," and more. They're like the glue that holds sentences together, but sometimes they can be super tricky, especially when English isn't your first language. No worries, though, this post will break it down for you and help you avoid some common mistakes.

Prepositions and Location:

Think of prepositions as giving directions. They tell us where something is. Here's the lowdown:

  • "In": Use this for larger areas or enclosed spaces:
    • "I live in Australia."
    • "The meeting is in the conference room."
    • "I put the milk in the fridge."
  • "At": Use this for specific points or locations:
    • "I'm at the library."
    • "The accident happened at the intersection."
    • "Meet me at the entrance."
  • "On": Use this for surfaces:
    • "The book is on the table."
    • "The picture hangs on the wall."
    • "I'm sitting on the floor."

Common Mistakes with Locations:

  • Mixing up "in" and "at" for locations: Saying "in the restaurant" when you should say "at the restaurant." "In" usually implies inside, while "at" suggests a specific point within the restaurant.
  • Using "in" instead of "on" for surfaces: Saying "The papers are in the table" instead of "on the table."

Prepositions and Time:

Prepositions can also tell us when something happens:

  • "In": Use this for months, years, seasons, and general parts of the day:
    • "I'm graduating in May."
    • "I was born in 1999."
    • "The flowers bloom in spring."
    • "I usually wake up early in the morning."
  • "At": Use this for specific times, holidays, and specific points in the day:
    • "The meeting is at 3:00 p.m."
    • "I'm going home at Christmas."
    • "I eat lunch at noon."
  • "On": Use this for days and specific dates:
    • "My birthday is on Saturday."
    • "The exam is on March 15th."

Common Mistakes with Time:

  • Using "on" instead of "in" for months and years: Saying "I was born on 1999" instead of "in 1999."
  • Using "at" instead of "on" for days: Saying "The party is at Friday" instead of "on Friday."

Prepositions and Movement:

Some prepositions describe movement. The most common one is "to":

  • "To": Use this to show direction:
    • "I'm going to school."
    • "She walked to the park."
    • "We flew to London."
  • Using “to” after verbs that don’t require it, such as ‘discuss’ and ‘explain:’
    • "I need to discuss about my scholarship." should be "I need to discuss my scholarship."
    • "Could you explain to me this question?" should be "Could you explain this question?"

More Tricky Prepositions and Common Errors:

Here are some other prepositions that often trip people up:

  • "For": Use this to indicate duration or purpose:
    • "I studied for three hours." (duration)
    • "This gift is for you." (purpose)
  • "Since": Use this to indicate a point in time from which something continues:
    • "I've been working here since 2020."
  • "With": Use this to indicate accompaniment or togetherness:
    • "I'm going to the movies with my friends."

Common Mistake: Using “for" instead of “since” with time expressions. “I’ve been living in Australia for last year.” should be “I’ve been living in Australia since last year.”

Just remember, practice makes perfect! The more you use prepositions in context, the better you'll get at them. Good luck, and keep up the great work on your English language journey! And remember, asking questions is important. So please ask if anything isn't clear!
